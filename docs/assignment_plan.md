# Found-Footage Video Essay Work Plan

> **Academic integrity note:** The assignment states: “Do not use generative AI to write the script, produce the voiceover, select the clips, or assemble the video.” This file therefore does **not** provide a finished script, chosen clip list, voiceover, or edited video. It provides a workflow, source links, decision templates, and technical tools so the student can complete those required authorship tasks independently.

## Assignment constraints captured from screenshots

- Make a found-footage video essay with voiceover narration.
- Use one central Freudian concept, such as repression, dream-work, displacement, condensation, slips, return of the repressed, the uncanny, or conflict within the psyche.
- Interpret something specific in media rather than explaining Freud only in the abstract.
- Use found material only: short clips from existing media, not newly recorded footage.
- Editing should carry part of the argument through montage, juxtaposition, repetition, interruption, speed changes, or sonic layering.
- Target length: about 2 minutes; acceptable range is **1:45–2:15**.
- Narration, analysis, clip selection, voiceover, and assembly must be the student's own work.
- Submission post should include the embedded video and a one-sentence thesis statement.

## Recommended completion path

1. Pick **one** Freudian concept.
2. Write a one-sentence thesis in your own words.
3. Choose 4–8 short found-footage moments that repeat, contradict, disguise, or reveal the chosen concept.
4. Draft a 250–330 word voiceover in your own words. At a typical narration pace, this fits roughly two minutes.
5. Build a rough edit with the voiceover first, then place footage against it.
6. Add captions from the final narration text.
7. Export an MP4 between 1:45 and 2:15.
8. Upload/embed through Kaltura, then verify video, audio, and captions play.

## Editing structure template

| Timecode | Function | What the student decides |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00–0:15 | Hook | A striking recurring image, line, gesture, or contradiction from the selected media |
| 0:15–0:30 | Thesis | The one-sentence interpretive claim |
| 0:30–1:15 | Evidence pattern 1 | 2–3 clips that show the surface meaning and the hidden/repressed meaning |
| 1:15–1:45 | Evidence pattern 2 | Repetition, interruption, slow motion, or juxtaposition to develop the argument |
| 1:45–2:05 | Return/conclusion | The opening image returns with a changed meaning |
| 2:05–2:15 | Credits | Brief source credits and attribution |

## Safe public-domain / reusable footage starting points

These are starting places to browse. The student should make final clip choices independently.

- Internet Archive, *Television Commercials (1950s–1960s)* — public-domain usage label; useful for consumer desire, domestic ideals, repression, substitution, and contradiction: https://archive.org/details/Televisi1960
- Internet Archive, *Classic Television Commercials* — public-domain usage label; useful for repetition, commodity fantasy, gendered performance, and hidden anxieties: https://archive.org/details/ctvc
- Internet Archive, *Classic Television Commercials (Part IV)* — public-domain usage label; useful for historical advertising montage: https://archive.org/details/ClassicT1948_4
- Library of Congress, *Public Domain Films from the National Film Registry* — public-domain films selected for free use and reuse: https://www.loc.gov/free-to-use/public-domain-films-from-the-national-film-registry/
- Library of Congress, *National Screening Room* — downloadable public-domain and rights-cleared moving-image materials; verify each item’s rights statement before use: https://www.loc.gov/collections/national-screening-room/
- Public Domain Review, *Prelinger Archives* overview — context for Prelinger’s ephemeral films, home movies, educational films, and advertising films: https://publicdomainreview.org/collections/source/prelinger-archives/

## Possible concepts to consider independently

- **The uncanny:** ordinary spaces or objects become strange through repetition, doubling, mechanical behavior, or forced cheerfulness.
- **Displacement:** emotional conflict appears indirectly through substitute objects, gestures, commodities, or scenes.
- **Condensation:** multiple desires or anxieties are compressed into one image, product, slogan, or repeated shot.
- **Return of the repressed:** something excluded or hidden comes back through a glitch, repeated image, background detail, or contradiction.
